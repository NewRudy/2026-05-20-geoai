\title{How Robust Is Multitemporal Sentinel-1/Sentinel-2 Fusion for Flood Mapping under Optical Modality Degradation?}

\author[aff1]{Author Name}
\affiliation[aff1]{organization={Affiliation to be confirmed}}

\begin{abstract}
Multitemporal Sentinel-1/Sentinel-2 fusion can improve supervised flood mapping,
but disaster response often faces incomplete or degraded optical observations.
This study evaluates whether clean benchmark evaluation overstates the
reliability of multimodal flood segmentation when optical inputs are missing or
corrupted. Using the public OMBRIA dataset, we train lightweight multitemporal
U-Net baselines and stress-test multimodal checkpoints under controlled
Sentinel-2 degradation. A simple training-time S2 degradation strategy improves
robustness when all optical channels are removed (IoU +0.2698, F1 +0.3728) and
when after-event S2 is replaced by noise (IoU +0.0780, F1 +0.0888), but it
slightly reduces clean performance (IoU -0.0552) and does not improve the
patch-masking condition. The results support a robustness-tradeoff view of
SAR-optical fusion: multimodal models can be made less brittle to missing or
corrupted optical inputs, but the benefit is degradation-specific and should not
be reported as universal robustness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Rapid flood extent mapping is an important remote-sensing application because
flood boundaries can change quickly and field observations are often sparse
during disaster response. Sentinel-1 synthetic aperture radar (SAR) is useful in
this setting because it can observe the surface through cloud cover, while
Sentinel-2 optical imagery provides complementary spectral information when the
scene is observable. Multitemporal SAR-optical fusion is therefore attractive:
pre-event and post-event observations from both sensors can encode inundation,
land-cover context, and change information.

However, standard supervised benchmarks usually evaluate models on clean,
paired inputs. This setting can hide a practical weakness. During real flood
events, after-event optical imagery may be unavailable, cloud-contaminated,
partly invalid, or otherwise misaligned with the conditions assumed during
training. A model that appears strong under clean S1/S2 fusion may therefore be
less reliable when the optical stream is degraded.

This paper studies that reliability boundary using OMBRIA, a public
multitemporal Sentinel-1/Sentinel-2 flood mapping dataset introduced with
OmbriaNet \cite{drakonakis2022ombrianet}. Instead of proposing a large new
architecture, we ask a narrower question: how much does a lightweight multimodal
flood segmentation baseline depend on clean Sentinel-2 inputs, and can simple
training-time optical degradation reduce that dependence?

The contribution is threefold:

\begin{itemize}
  \item We evaluate multimodal OMBRIA flood segmentation under controlled
  Sentinel-2 degradation modes, separating clean benchmark performance from
  degraded-input reliability.
  \item We test a lightweight S2 degradation training baseline that randomly
  exposes the model to clean, missing, noisy, and patch-masked optical inputs.
  \item We report both positive and negative results, showing strong robustness
  gains for missing/corrupted optical inputs but a measurable clean-performance
  penalty and a failure to improve the patch-masking case.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

OmbriaNet introduced OMBRIA as a supervised flood mapping benchmark using
multitemporal Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 data fusion
\cite{drakonakis2022ombrianet}. The dataset and reference experiments make it a
suitable base for a small, reproducible robustness study because S1 and S2
imagery are already paired for before-event and after-event observations.

Several studies have examined flood mapping across sensor modalities.
Cross-modal distillation has been proposed to transfer information between
optical and SAR flood mapping models \cite{garg2023crossmodal}, reflecting the
broader observation that each modality has different strengths and failure
modes. More generally, incomplete multimodal learning for remote sensing studies
how models should behave when one or more modalities are missing at inference
time \cite{chen2023incomplete}. Our work is aligned with this literature but
keeps the method deliberately simple: we test whether basic degradation-aware
training is enough to reveal and reduce optical-input brittleness in an OMBRIA
flood mapping setting.

Recent GeoAI and remote-sensing foundation model work has emphasized larger
pretrained models and generalist Earth observation representations
\cite{jakubik2023foundation,huang2025survey,marsocci2025terratorch}. Those
models are important, but the present study focuses on a smaller question that
can be tested under limited compute: whether conventional multimodal fusion
evaluation itself is robust to missing or degraded optical channels.

\section{Data}

We use the public OMBRIA repository data. The processed subset used here
contains paired Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 before-event and after-event chips for
624 training chips and 70 test chips. The original training split is divided
into 530 training chips and 94 validation chips using a fixed random seed. Each
sample provides before-event S1, after-event S1, before-event S2, after-event
S2, and a binary flood mask. The multimodal input concatenates S2 before, S2
after, S1 before, and S1 after channels.

The controlled S2 degradation modes are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{none}: clean S1/S2 input.
  \item \textbf{zero\_after}: after-event S2 channels are set to zero.
  \item \textbf{zero\_all}: both before-event and after-event S2 channels are
  set to zero.
  \item \textbf{noise\_after}: after-event S2 channels are replaced by random
  noise.
  \item \textbf{patch\_after}: after-event S2 is partially masked by zero-valued
  patches.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methods}

The segmentation model is a compact U-Net-style convolutional network with
three encoder blocks, a bottleneck, and three decoder blocks. This architecture
is intentionally lightweight so that repeated-seed experiments can run on a
free Colab/T4 GPU. The model is trained with binary cross-entropy on flood masks
and evaluated using IoU, F1, precision, recall, and accuracy.

The main comparison is between two multimodal training regimes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Clean training}: all training samples use clean S1/S2 inputs.
  \item \textbf{S2 degradation training}: each training sample randomly uses
  clean S2, zeroed after-event S2, zeroed all S2, noisy after-event S2, or
  patch-masked after-event S2.
\end{itemize}

Both checkpoints are evaluated under the same test-time degradation modes. This
design isolates the effect of training-time degradation from the effect of
test-time optical corruption.

\section{Experimental Design}

The current experiment uses three seeds: 7, 13, and 21. Each model is trained
for 25 epochs with batch size 8 and 16 base channels. For each seed, a clean
multimodal checkpoint and an S2-degradation-trained multimodal checkpoint are
trained. Each checkpoint is then reloaded and evaluated under the five S2
conditions listed above.

The primary metrics are mean test IoU and mean test F1 across the three seeds.
We report the difference between S2 degradation training and clean training for
each test condition. Positive values indicate that degradation-aware training
improves robustness under that condition.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness} summarizes the final matrix. Clean training performs
best on clean inputs, with mean IoU 0.6890 and mean F1 0.8092. S2 degradation
training reduces clean IoU by 0.0552 and clean F1 by 0.0418, showing a
non-negligible accuracy cost.

The same training strategy is beneficial when S2 is missing or strongly
corrupted. Under \texttt{zero\_all}, clean training nearly collapses
(IoU 0.0406), while S2 degradation training reaches IoU 0.3104. Under
\texttt{noise\_after}, IoU improves from 0.3285 to 0.4065. Under
\texttt{zero\_after}, the gain is smaller but still positive.

The patch-masking result is the important negative case. Under
\texttt{patch\_after}, S2 degradation training slightly lowers IoU from 0.5722
to 0.5616. Therefore, the current result does not justify a claim of universal
robustness across optical degradation types.

\section{Discussion}

The results support the central premise that clean multimodal benchmark
evaluation can overstate practical reliability. Clean S1/S2 fusion is effective
when all modalities are available, but performance can drop sharply when optical
channels are removed or corrupted. A simple degradation-aware training schedule
partly mitigates this brittleness, especially when the model must fall back to
S1 information after S2 removal.

The clean-performance penalty matters. A method that improves degraded
conditions by sacrificing too much clean accuracy may not be desirable when
high-quality optical imagery is available. The current penalty is slightly above
the planned 0.05 IoU threshold, so the result should be framed as a tradeoff
rather than a finished robust-fusion solution.

The patch-masking failure suggests that the schedule does not yet teach the
model to handle local optical occlusion as effectively as global missingness or
random corruption. A focused follow-up should test lighter degradation schedules
to reduce the clean penalty and patch-aware schedules to address local
occlusion.

This study is also limited by its single-dataset scope and synthetic
degradation design. The degradation modes are controlled and reproducible, but
they are not a full physical simulation of cloud cover, atmospheric effects, or
sensor-specific invalid observations. Future work should evaluate event-level
generalization and real optical quality masks when available.

\section{Conclusion}

This work provides an initial robustness-tradeoff analysis of multitemporal
Sentinel-1/Sentinel-2 flood mapping on OMBRIA. S2 degradation training improves
model behavior when optical inputs are missing or corrupted, especially when all
S2 channels are removed, but it reduces clean performance and does not improve a
patch-masking stress test. The current evidence is strong enough to continue the
paper route, provided that the final claim remains specific: robustness gains
are real for missing/corrupted optical inputs, but they are not universal across
all degradation types.
